How to read this investigation

Each of the six knowledge areas below follows the same structure: a short hypothesis label names the concern, the full knowledge question frames it, hypotheses describe what good and poor performance looks like, information questions specify what data to collect, data sources identify where to find it, and a recommendation states what to do if the hypothesis is confirmed. Every element traces back to the wisdom question above.

01
Knowledge area 01
Win Rate Collapsing
Are we losing deals we should be winning?
Hypotheses
We lose on relationships — competitors have stronger site presence between RFQs and our teams are not visible enough between tender cycles.
We lose on commercial terms — our pricing or payment conditions are consistently outside what customers will accept relative to competitor offers.
We lose on proposal quality — competitors articulate value more clearly and customers cannot justify the premium without supporting evidence.
Information questions
What is our win rate by product line and customer segment over the last 24 months?
At which stage of the sales cycle are most deals lost — qualification, proposal, or commercial negotiation?
What reasons do customers give when we debrief lost tenders — and how do these differ by product line?
How does our win rate compare against our top two competitors on the same opportunities?
Data sources
CRM — all closed opportunities (won & lost) past 24 months
Tender log — RFQ participation, submission, and outcome records
Customer exit interviews and lost-tender debrief notes
Competitor intelligence reports and market pricing signals
Recommendation
Implement structured win/loss debriefs on every lost deal above threshold value. Route findings into monthly commercial review. Salespeople cannot improve what they do not systematically track.
02
Knowledge area 02
Wrong Product Mix
Is our product mix aligned with market demand?
Hypotheses
The market has shifted toward smaller, higher-utilisation fleets — our product range is weighted toward large capital equipment that fewer customers are currently buying.
Certain product lines are growing in the market but are under-represented in our active sales effort and customer conversations.
Our product development cycle is slower than customer adoption patterns — by the time we bring solutions to market, customers have committed to alternatives.
Information questions
Which of our product lines have grown, held, or declined in market share over the past 3 years?
What is the revenue mix across equipment, rebuilds, components, and services — and how has this shifted?
Which commodity cycles (gold, copper, coal, PGMs) are currently driving customer capex decisions?
What product lines are our competitors winning with that we do not carry or do not actively promote?
Data sources
ERP / order management — revenue by product line, region, and period
Market research reports — OEM market share by equipment segment
Commodity price & capex cycle data from public mining company reports
Competitor product catalogues and observed tender wins
Recommendation
Rank product lines by margin contribution and strategic fit. Redirect sales effort away from structurally declining lines. Retire or bundle lines with persistent low volume and margin drag.
03
Knowledge area 03
Key Accounts Spending Less
Are key accounts reducing spend with us?
Hypotheses
Spend decline is concentrated in a small number of large accounts — the broader customer base is stable and the problem is account-specific, not systemic.
Customers are consolidating supplier lists and we are being de-selected in favour of OEMs who offer broader service bundles or more integrated support.
Relationship continuity has broken down — account ownership has changed hands and trust built with senior mine leadership has not transferred.
Information questions
Which accounts show the largest year-on-year revenue decline — and when exactly did the decline begin?
What is our share of wallet within each key account — what are they buying from competitors that they previously bought from us?
How frequently are our salespeople visiting key accounts, and at what seniority level (procurement, engineering, operations, C-suite)?
Have there been significant changes in account ownership or mine management in the last 18 months?
Data sources
ERP — revenue per account, product line breakdown, and YoY delta
CRM — account visit logs, contact records, and relationship mapping
Customer spend reports where available from account reviews
HR and account management handover records
Recommendation
Identify the top 10 accounts by spend reduction. Assign senior account owners. Conduct relationship audits within 30 days. Quantify what it would take to recover each account before assuming it is lost.
04
Knowledge area 04
Sales Activity Misdirected
Are salespeople targeting the right opportunities at the right time?
Hypotheses
Our salespeople are active but targeting the wrong accounts — spending time on low-probability or low-value prospects while high-value accounts are underserved.
Sales effort is concentrated on new equipment deals because the commission structure rewards capital sales over aftermarket, leaving rebuild and component revenue unsold.
There is no systematic prospecting against fleet age data — salespeople are reactive to inbound enquiries rather than proactively building pipeline from installed base intelligence.
Information questions
How is each salesperson's time allocated across new equipment, components, rebuilds, and services in an average month?
What is the average pipeline value per salesperson, and how does conversion rate vary between individuals?
Are salespeople using fleet age and installed base data to prospect for rebuild and component opportunities?
What does the incentive structure reward — and does it create a bias toward capital sales at the expense of aftermarket revenue?
Data sources
CRM — activity logs, pipeline by stage, product line, and salesperson
Incentive and commission scheme documentation
Installed base register — fleet age, hours, and service history per unit
Sales manager call reports and territory reviews
Recommendation
Build a prospecting calendar from installed base records and make rebuild conversations mandatory on every site visit. Review the incentive structure to reward aftermarket as a primary, not secondary, revenue target.
05
Knowledge area 05
Price & Value Perception Gap
Is pricing or value perception driving customers away?
Hypotheses
Customers are not walking away on price alone — they are walking away because they cannot quantify the value difference between us and cheaper alternatives.
Our list pricing is above market on certain product lines and sales teams are discounting to close, eroding margin without meaningfully improving win rate.
Total cost of ownership (TCO) conversations are not happening — customers compare unit price in isolation and our premium is indefensible without uptime and maintenance cost data.
Information questions
What is the average discount applied at close, by product line and by salesperson?
In deals where we lost on price, how large was the gap — and how often does it fall within our discount authority?
Do customers who bought on lowest unit price return for components and rebuilds at the same rate as full-value customers?
Are sales teams equipped with TCO models and trained to use them in customer conversations?
Data sources
ERP / quote system — list price vs invoice price, discount % by product line
CRM — lost deal pricing notes and competitor price intelligence
Customer lifecycle revenue data — capex vs aftermarket spend per unit over life
Sales training records — TCO tool adoption and usage frequency
Recommendation
Develop TCO tools for the three highest-volume product lines. Train sales teams to lead with cost-per-tonne and uptime metrics rather than unit price. Price defensibility follows value clarity — not deeper discounts.
06
Knowledge area 06
Aftermarket Revenue Untapped
Is the aftermarket being actively and effectively sold?
Hypotheses
Aftermarket is under-captured because it is treated as a service function rather than a sales function — no explicit targets, no pipeline, no accountability structure.
Customers are sourcing components and rebuild work from third-party suppliers and local workshops — often because our lead times or parts pricing are not competitive.
We do not have reliable visibility into our installed base — salespeople cannot proactively target rebuild cycles because they do not know which units are approaching service intervals.
Information questions
What percentage of our installed base is active on our components and service contracts — and how has this trended over time?
What is the average aftermarket revenue generated per unit over the equipment lifecycle?
Where are customers going for components and rebuilds when they are not coming to us — and what are their stated reasons?
Do we have a complete, current record of every unit in the field — its age, hours, and next major service milestone?
Data sources
Installed base register — unit records, service history, and contract status
ERP — aftermarket revenue by account, product line, and quarter
Service and parts orders — fill rate, lead time, and third-party leakage signals
Customer interviews on sourcing decisions for parts and rebuild work
Recommendation
Set explicit aftermarket revenue targets per salesperson. Include components and services in all major account reviews. Consider separating aftermarket as a dedicated sales motion with its own pipeline and incentive structure.

Answering the wisdom question

With the investigation complete, we return to the original question. Each recommendation below is traceable to a knowledge area, a set of hypotheses, and specific data. No recommendation stands alone.

What we should do

Six prioritised actions to reverse the sales decline

REC 01 — Win rate
Fix the pipeline before chasing volume
Structured win/loss debriefs on every lost deal above threshold. Route findings into monthly commercial review. You cannot improve what you do not track.
REC 02 — Product mix
Rebalance effort toward high-margin product lines
Rank product lines by margin and strategic fit. Redirect effort from structurally declining lines. Retire or bundle low-volume, low-margin products.
REC 03 — Key accounts
Launch an account recovery programme
Identify top 10 accounts by spend reduction. Assign senior owners. Conduct relationship audits within 30 days before writing off any account.
REC 04 — Sales activity
Redirect activity toward rebuild and component cycles
Build a prospecting calendar from installed base data. Make rebuild conversations mandatory on every site visit. Review what the incentive structure actually rewards.
REC 05 — Pricing
Strengthen value articulation, not just pricing
Develop TCO tools for the three highest-volume lines. Train teams to lead with cost-per-tonne and uptime. Price defensibility follows value clarity.
REC 06 — Aftermarket
Make aftermarket a primary revenue target
Set explicit aftermarket targets per salesperson. Include components and services in all account reviews. Consider a dedicated aftermarket sales motion.
